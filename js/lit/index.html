🌸︎ higasi

higasi (ಬಿಗಸಿ) is the primary language of the peninsular country of higasi oka (ಬಿಗಸಿ ಒಕ), whose territory extends to a number of islands to the northeast of the østendūnska imperium. the language, culture, and much of the geography of higasi oka is based on our world's japan; however, the writing system is instead that of kannada, from the indian state of karnataka.

while this is an instance of using a real-world language to save effort on constructing languages, some alterations are still made to give higasi its own identity apart from japanese. first, the pronunciation is based on the underlying phonemes, rather than dialectical phonetics; e.g. ‹ちゃ› is pronounced as /tja/ rather than the [tɕa] realized via yod-coalescence in the "standard" tokyo dialect. secondly, kanji are, whenever possible, read as their kun'yomi; e.g. the compound ｢絶対｣ would normally be read as ｢ぜったい｣ (zettai) based on its on'yomi, but here it's read as ｢たあいて｣ (taaite). finally, as the stories in this world are set in eras approximately equivalent to the 17th to early 20th centuries, historic vocabulary is preferable... though that could just be my late onset 中二病 (here read as ｢なかふたや｣, where the ｢ふ｣ is obviously pronounced as /hu/ rather than [ɸɯ]).

as for the mapping to the kannada script, this is relatively straightforward. there were a few missing phonemes, of course, but most deviations from the phonetics of kannada were done for the sake of aesthetics:

  1. the diacritic form of ‹ಒ› (o) is a bit overbearing, e.g. 'ಕೊ' (ko), so it is substituted with ‹ಔ› (au), e.g. 'ಕೌ' (kau), and is instead used as the diacritic form of ‹ಓ› (ō), e.g. 'ಕೊ' (kō).
  2. kannada lacks the /z/ consonant, so ‹ಶ› (śa) is used.
  3. for consistent styling, the character ‹ಹ› (ha) is substituted with ‹ಬ› (ba) and ‹ಭ› (bha) is then used for /ba/.
  4. compound forms of ‹ರ› (ra) with ‹ಯ› (ya) render incorrectly (in noto fonts), so it is substituted with ‹ಠ› (ṭha), e.g. 'ಠ್ಯ.' this also gives and eye to 'ಠ್ಯೂ' (ryuu), so it's cool.
  5. to clarify ‹ん› (n) as being without a vowel, ‹ಙ› (ṅa) is used. alternatively, a virama could have been used, i.e. ‹ನ್›, but using a unique base grapheme better reflects its unique nature as a kana.

not included in this chart is the treatment of the geminating prefix, sokuon ‹っ›, which is transliterated as the kannada yōgavāha (semi-vocalic) ‹Oಃ › (ḥ).

limitiations:

  • unlike liturgical lilhian, the inputs are not bidirectional, each is processed independently and only shares the output(s), i.e. the latin input will not affect the japanese input and vise versa.
  • gemination may not always be accurately matched, given the complications involved in detecting syllables to attach the diacritic to for both latin and japanese input. the regexp used for these is, respectively: 
    // latin
[/([kgszjtcdnhbpmr]){2,}(h?y?(aa?|ii?|uu?|ee?|o[ou]?))/gi, '$1$2\u0c38']
// japanese
[new RegExp(
  '(っ)+(' +
  '([かカがガさサざザたタだダなナはハばバぱパまマらラ]|'+
    '[きキぎギしシじジちチにニひヒびビぴピみミりリ][ゃャ]|ヴァ)[あアー]?|'+
  '([くクぐグすスずズつツづヅぬヌふフぶブヴぷプむムるル]|'+
    '[きキぎギしシじジちチにニひヒびビぴピみミりリ][ゅュ])[うウー]?|'+
  '([けケげゲせセぜゼてテでデねネへヘべベぺペめメれレ]|ヴェ)[えエー]?|'+
  '([こコごゴそソぞゾとトどドのノほホぼボぽポもモろロ]|'+
    '[きキぎギしシじジちチにニひヒびビぴピみミりリ][ょョ])[おオうウー]?|'+
  '([きキぎギしシじジちチぢヂにニひヒびビぴピみミりリ]|ヴィ)[いイー]?)'
, 'g'), '$2\u0c83']
    most obviously, for the latin input, ANY consecutive consonants may be treated as gemination!
  • higasi should, like kannada, use spaces between words; however, it is arguably impossible to determine japanese word boundaries with regular expressions alone. spaces must then be input by the user to be rendered in the output.
  • lengthening vowels with small kana, e.g. ｢おぅ｣, is not supported; please use either full kana, e.g. ｢おう｣, or the tyôonpu, e.g. ｢オー｣.
  • usage of the sokuon ‹っ› for emphatic sentence endings, i.e. glottal stops, is not supported.
  • only 2026 kanji are supported, taken from the wanikani set of kanji-data from davidluzgouveia. the data was further processed for simplification by the following script: 
    GENKI = j =>
  Object.keys(j).map(i =>
    [i, (j[i].readings_kun[0] || j[i].readings_on[0])
    .replace(/\.(.*)/g, '').replace('-', '')]
  ),
    as this only takes the first kun'yomi (or on'yomi when unavailable), it lacks any sort of artistic oversight and should be taken only as reference.

given the nature of kannada as an abugida, extending cozette to render it would be non-trivial. therefore, and additional font has been added here: bengaluru from akshara type studio. this font does, however, have a minor rendering issue; the control points are not perfectly aligned to the pixel grid (mostly being 1/64 off) and thus in some instances the vertical stems may gain some antialiasing on either side.

💯︎ østendunska numerals

in the realm of wydheth, the østendūna imperium reigns over the heart of a vast and mountainous continent. while the imperium is home to many tongues, they all share a common script and, by extension, numeral system.

this system is, in some ways, similar to our world's roman numerals. particularly in the repetition of characters to represent multiples of their values, e.g. "ii" for two; as well as using the apostrophus method for large numbers, e.g. "cıɔ" for a thousand. they diverge in that østendūna traditionally uses base-6 counting rather than base-10, thus there is little need for subtractive notation, e.g. "iv" for four.

the symbols for numbers are mostly taken from the østendun alphabet, though 36 and 108 are modified to reflect their values:
